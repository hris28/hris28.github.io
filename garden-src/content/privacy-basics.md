# Privacy Basics

Privacy is not only about hiding information. It is about having appropriate control over how information about you is collected, used, shared, retained, and interpreted.

## A practical privacy model

Ask these questions:

| Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| What data is collected? | You cannot protect what you do not know exists |
| Who can access it? | Access determines risk |
| Why is it collected? | Some collection is necessary; some is excessive |
| How long is it kept? | Long retention increases exposure |
| Can it be deleted? | Deletion rights vary by service and jurisdiction |
| Can it be combined with other data? | Small details can become revealing when aggregated |

## Low-effort privacy improvements

- Use unique passwords.
- Enable multifactor authentication.
- Remove unused apps and browser extensions.
- Review social media visibility settings.
- Turn off unnecessary location access.
- Use encrypted messaging when appropriate.
- Be careful posting schedules, addresses, IDs, and travel plans.

## Privacy is contextual

The right privacy choice depends on the situation. A journalist, student, researcher, activist, system administrator, and casual user may all have different threat models.
